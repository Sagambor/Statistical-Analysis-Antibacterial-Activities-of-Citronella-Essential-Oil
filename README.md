# Statistical-Analysis-Antibacterial-Activities-of-Citronella-Essential-Oil
A statistical analysis project examining the antibacterial efficacy of citronella essential oil (Cymbopogon nardus) against Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus. This analysis uses SPSS one-way ANOVA and post-hoc testing to evaluate experimental data.
Research Context
This analysis examines experimental data investigating the potential of citronella essential oil as a natural antimicrobial agent against antibiotic-resistant bacteria. The research was motivated by the growing global crisis of antimicrobial resistance, where bacteria such as Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus have developed resistance to conventional antibiotics.
According to the WHO (2020), antimicrobial resistance has led to over 700,000 deaths annually, with projections reaching 10 million deaths per year by 2050 if no action is taken. This underscores the urgent need to investigate alternative antimicrobial agents derived from natural sources.
Plant of Interest: Citronella (Cymbopogon nardus)
Citronella is a tall aromatic grass with documented traditional medicinal uses. The essential oil contains bioactive compounds including citronellal, geraniol, and limonene that have shown promise as antimicrobial agents. Despite traditional knowledge supporting its antibacterial properties, there is limited systematic analysis of its efficacy against common pathogenic bacteria.

Escherichia coli (Gram-negative)

Staphylococcus aureus (Gram-positive)

Research Objectives
What the Study Aimed to Achieve
The experimental study sought to:

Determine the antibacterial activities of citronella essential oil extract against Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus
Assess whether the oil's concentration affects its antibacterial potency through measurement of zones of inhibition
Compare which bacterial species is more susceptible to citronella oil treatment
Evaluate the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) at different dilution levels

One-way ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) was chosen for this analysis because:

Multiple Groups Comparison: We had 4 groups to compare (positive control, 50%, 25%, 12.5% concentrations), and ANOVA is designed for comparing means across three or more groups
Testing Overall Significance: ANOVA tests whether there is ANY significant difference among all groups simultaneously—more efficient than conducting multiple t-tests (which would inflate Type I error)
Addressing the Research Question: The study aimed to determine if concentration affects antibacterial activity. ANOVA directly answers whether mean zone of inhibition differs significantly across concentrations
Avoiding Multiple Comparison Errors: Conducting pairwise t-tests between all groups would require multiple comparisons, increasing the risk of false positives. ANOVA controls for this
Post-Hoc Testing Capability: After finding overall significance with ANOVA, post-hoc tests (Tukey HSD) identify exactly which groups differ from each other

Hypotheses:

Null Hypothesis (H₀): There is no significant difference in mean zone of inhibition across different citronella oil concentrations
Alternative Hypothesis (Hₐ): There is a significant difference in mean zone of inhibition across different citronella oil concentrations

Key Findings
Concentration-Dependent Antibacterial Activity
The statistical analysis confirms that citronella oil's antibacterial potency is directly related to its concentration. Each step increase in concentration (12.5% → 25% → 50%) produces a statistically significant increase in the zone of inhibition.
Differential Bacterial Susceptibility
The raw data showed that Staphylococcus aureus (Gram-positive) was consistently more susceptible to citronella oil than Escherichia coli (Gram-negative):

S. aureus average at 50%: 15.0 mm
E. coli average at 50%: 12.5 mm

This difference is consistent with biological theory: Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer that allows easier penetration by hydrophobic compounds, while Gram-negative bacteria have an outer membrane that acts as a barrier.

Practical Minimum Inhibitory Concentration
The statistical analysis shows that the 50% concentration is significantly more effective than lower concentrations. Even the 12.5% concentration showed measurable antibacterial activity (7.5-8.5 mm), though 25% and 12.5% were not significantly different from each other, suggesting a practical MIC somewhere between 12.5% and 25%.
Statistical Rigor
The ANOVA analysis with p = 0.000 provides very strong evidence that these effects are real and reproducible, not artifacts of the small sample size or experimental variation.

Summary
This SPSS analysis demonstrates that citronella essential oil exhibits a statistically significant, concentration-dependent antibacterial effect against both E. coli and S. aureus, with greater efficacy against the Gram-positive species. The one-way ANOVA was the appropriate statistical tool to analyze whether concentration differences produced meaningful variations in antibacterial activity, and the results strongly support the original research hypothesis that citronella oil has therapeutic potential as a natural antimicrobial agent.
